Cameras and Lenses

1. Recording Light with Sensors

Digital image sensors consists of a grid of photodetectors that convert photons into electric current that can be measured. The more photons hitting the detector the higher the signal.

In the demonstration below you can see how photons would fall onto the arrangement of photodetectors represented by small squares. The slider controls the flow of time.

As you can see, the longer the time of collection of photons the more of them are hitting the detectors and the brighter the resulting pixels in the image. When we don't gather enough photons the image is underexposed, but if we allow the photon collection to run for too long the image will be overexposed.

It might surprise you that the resulting image is black and white, even though the photons have the "color" of their wavelength, the photodetectors don't see that color. The photodetectors only measure the total intensity which results in a black and white image. To record the color information we need to separate the incoming photons into distinct groups. We can put tiny color filters on top of the detectors so that they will only accept, more or less, red, green, or blue light. The most common filter arrangement is the Bayer filter pattern:

The Bayer filter pattern consists of a 2x2 grid of two green filters, one red filter, and one blue filter. There are other filter arrangements, like the X-Trans, but the Bayer pattern is the most common in digital cameras. The filters let only photons of a specific color through to the detector underneath:

This is what an image looks like when captured with a Bayer filter:

As you can see, the resulting image is very pixelated and clearly shows the Bayer filter pattern. To get a clean image we need to perform a process called demosaicing that estimates the missing color information for each pixel based on the surrounding pixels. The result is a much smoother image:

3. Pinhole Camera

We need to have a method to control the light hitting the sensor. Let's put the sensor in a box that has a small hole in it. The first slider controls the diameter of the hole, while the second one controls the distance between the opening and the sensor:

We've just built a pinhole camera! The image is inverted as the rays cross over in the hole and the formed image is a horizontal and a vertical reflection of the actual scene. This is illustrated in the following two figures.

We define the frustum of a camera as the volume of space that is visible to the camera. The frustum is defined by the field of view, the near and far clipping planes, and the aspect ratio of the image (or sensor).

In a pinhole camera, the sharpness of an image depends on the size of the hole. A smaller hole produces a sharper image but less light is able to pass through it.

Figure 15 - Solid angle of pinhole

The following figure shows what happens at the sensor level when the hole size is changed:

Until now, we have used an ideal sensor that can capture as much light as needed. If we would use a less ideal sensor, it would be able to capture only a limited amount of light. As you can see, when the hole is too large the rays from a single point in the scene are able to hit multiple pixels on the sensor, which results in a blurry image. When the hole is too small, the rays from a single point are only able to hit a single pixel, which results in a sharper image but less light is able to reach the sensor, which results in a darker image. To increase the amount of light, we could have large exposure times. However, this will cause motion blur in dynamic scenes.

4. Thin Lens Model

To solve the problems of the pinhole camera, we need a device that could solve both of these problems: a way to collect a bigger part of the light radiated by the objects and also control the sharpness of parts of the image. For the objects in the scene that must be sharp we want to collect as much of their light as possible, and make it converge to the smallest possible point. Then, if we place the sensor at this convergence point we will obtain a sharp image. The device that cameras use to achieve this is a lens. And the simplest lens that can achieve this is called a thin lens.

A thin lens is a piece of transparent material (e.g. glass) that has two spherical surfaces. It is an idealized lens, but it is commonly used to illustrate the principles of lens behavior. The following figure shows the geometry of a thin lens:

4.1 Focus and Bokeh

When the sensor is placed at the focal point of the lens, the image is sharp. However, when the sensor is moved away from the focal point, the rays from a single point in the scene are no longer able to converge to a single point on the sensor, which results in a blurry image. The amount of blur depends on how far the sensor is from the focal point. The following figures shows how the field of view changes when we move the sensor to keep an image sharp at different focal lengths:

4.2 Aperture

In cameras, there is another parameter that plays an important role on how much light is captured and how much of the scene is in focus. This parameter is called the aperture. The aperture is the opening in the lens that allows light to pass through. The size of the aperture affects both the amount of light that enters the camera and the so-called depth of field. Let's start by visualizing how the angle of the light of rays (light cone) can increase or reduce sharpness:

The aperture is controlled by the diaphragm of the lens, which consists of a set of blades that can open and close to adjust the size of the aperture. The shape of the aperture is determined by the number and shape of the blades. The more blades there are, the more circular the aperture will be, which results in smoother bokeh. The shape of the aperture also defines the shape of bokeh. This is the reason why bokeh sometimes has a polygonal shape - it's simply the shape of the "cone" of light after passing through the blades of the aperture.

The aperture has an effect on the light cone angle. A smaller aperture results in a smaller light cone angle, which increases the depth of field. A larger depth of field means that more parts of the scene are in focus. Conversely, a larger aperture results in a larger light cone angle, which decreases the depth of field.
